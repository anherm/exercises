\section*{Lattices and Convex Bodies: Exercises 3}

\begin{Ex}

Let $\Lambda \subset \R^d$ be a full-dimensional lattice, $y \in \Lambda^*\backslash 0$. Then $y$ is primitive if and only if every lattice hyperplane $\{x \in \Lambda \colon y^Tx = \alpha\}$ for $\alpha \in \Z$ contains a point of $\Lambda$.

\begin{proof}

First let $y \in \Lambda^*\backslash 0$ be primitive, i.\,e. for every $t \in (0, 1)$, we have $ty \notin \Lambda^*$. By definition of $\Lambda^*$, we know that for all $x \in \Lambda$, we have $y^Tx \in \Z$. Let

\[ A := \{ \alpha \in Z \colon x^Ty = \alpha \} \,.\]

We can choose some numeration $A = \{ \alpha_1, \alpha_2, \dotsc \}$ since it is a finite or countable subset of $\Z$, and we can choose $\alpha_1$ to be non-zero since if $A = \{0\}$, the whole lattice $\Lambda$ is orthogonal to $y$ and thus not full-dimensional. We define the greatest common divisor 
\[g := \text{gcd}(A) = \text{gcd} (\dotsc \text{gcd}(\alpha_3, \text{gcd}(\alpha_2, \alpha_1))\dotsc)\]

of $A$ recursively. Then $g \in \Z_{\geq 1}$ is well-defined although $A$ might be countable: Note that $g$ is bounded from above by $\text{gcd}(\alpha_1, \alpha_2) < \infty$ (as $\alpha_1 > 0)$, bounded from below by $1$, and that the greatest common divisor can only decrease when we add some $\alpha_{i+1}$ to a subset $\{\alpha_1, \dotsc, \alpha_i\}$ of $A$. Therefore there is an index $k \in \mathbb N$ with $\text{gcd}(A) = \text{gcd}(A_k)$ for $A_k = \{\alpha_1, \dotsc, \alpha_k\}$. 

Define $t := \frac{1}{g} \in (0, 1]$. Now let $x \in \Lambda$ and compute

\[ (ty)^Tx = t (y^Tx) = \frac{\alpha}{g} \in \Z\]

for some $\alpha \in \Z$ with $g | \alpha$. This means that $ty \in \Lambda^*$, and since $y$ was assumed to be primitive, we have $t = 1$ and thus $g = 1$. But then we can choose a linear combination $1 = \sum_{i = 1}^k \lambda_i \alpha_i$ with $\alpha_i \in Z$ defining some of the hit hyperplanes and $\lambda_i \in \Z$. If $x_i$ are vectors of $\Lambda$ with $y^T x_i = \alpha_i$, we can define $x := \sum_{i = 1}^k \lambda_i x_i \in \Z$ to obtain

\[ y^T x = \sum_{i = 1}^k \lambda_i y^T x = \sum_{i = 1}^k \lambda_i \alpha_i = 1 \,. \]

Thus the hyperplane defined by $\alpha = 1 \in \Z$ is hit, and by additivity, every other hyperplane is also hit.

On the other hand, let $y \in \Lambda^*\backslash 0$ be a vector such that for every $\alpha \in \Z$, we have 

\[\{x \in \Lambda \colon y^Tx = \alpha\} \cap \Lambda \neq \emptyset\,.\]

Assume there is some $t \in (0, 1)$ with $ty \in \Lambda^*$. Since every hyper plane is hit, there is some $x \in \Lambda$ with $y^Tx = 1$. But then we have 

\[ (ty)^Tx = t (y^Tx) = t \notin \Z \,,\]

contradicting to $ty \in \Lambda^*$. 
\end{proof}  

\end{Ex}
